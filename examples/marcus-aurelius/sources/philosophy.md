# Marcus Aurelius — Stoic Principles

Stoic philosophical foundations that underpin Marcus Aurelius's approach to leadership and self-governance.

---

## The Dichotomy of Control

The central Stoic distinction: focus entirely on what is within your power — your thoughts, judgments, choices — and regard everything else as indifferent. External things cannot harm your character unless you allow them to disturb your mind.

This is not passivity. It is the highest form of agency: refusing to waste energy on what you cannot change while exerting full effort on what you can.

---

## Virtue as the Highest Good

The only genuine good is virtue — wisdom, justice, courage, temperance. The only genuine evil is vice. Health, wealth, reputation, life itself are morally neutral; they become good or bad only through the use we make of them.

From this follows: judge actions by their rightness, not by their results. The virtuous person does not act for reward or recognition.

---

## Memento Mori — Contemplation of Death

Regular contemplation of mortality serves not to depress but to clarify. It strips away trivial concerns and reveals what actually matters. Every day should be lived as though it were the last — not from morbid fear but from urgent clarity about purpose.

---

## Amor Fati — Love of Fate

Not merely acceptance of what happens, but active love of it. Embrace your circumstances, including suffering and loss, as necessary parts of a larger pattern you cannot fully see. The obstacle is not something to be endured but something to be used.

---

## Duty and the Common Good

The rational being exists not for itself but for the whole. Each person has a role to play in the cosmic order. The emperor's duty is to govern justly, the philosopher's duty is to seek truth, the parent's duty is to nurture. Identify your role and fulfill it without attachment to outcome.

---

## Negative Visualization

Imagine the worst that could happen — the loss of what you most value. The purpose is not to court disaster but to prepare the mind. When the worst does not occur, gratitude deepens. When it does, you are not unprepared.

---

## The View From Above

When disturbed by petty concerns, zoom out. View yourself from the cosmic perspective — a speck in time, one among billions, brief and contingent. The concern dissolves in proportion. Then zoom back in with renewed purpose.

---

## Self-Examination

Examine your day: What did I do well? Where did I fall short? What can I correct tomorrow? This is not guilt but calibration. The unexamined life is not worth living, but examination without correction is merely self-torment.

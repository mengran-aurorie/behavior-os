# Sun Tzu — Strategic Principles from The Art of War

Selected passages on Sun Tzu's strategic philosophy, compiled from public domain translations.

---

## Core Strategic Principles

**Know yourself and your enemy, and you will never be defeated.**

The supreme art of war is not to fight battles, but to create conditions in which battle becomes unnecessary. The highest form of generalship is to balk the enemy's plans. The next best is to disrupt his alliances. The third is to attack his army in the field. The worst strategy is to besiege walled cities.

---

## On Deception and Misdirection

All warfare is based on deception. When we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when we are far away, we must make him believe we are near.

The art of war is the art of keeping the enemy in constant uncertainty and motion, destroying him through indirect means. He who occupies the ground first and awaits the enemy will be at ease; he who awaits the enemy at the ground he has chosen will be tired.

---

## On Strategic Advantage

He who can gain victory by strategic positioning does not require large numbers. The general who wins the battle has made many calculations before the engagement. The general who loses makes few calculations.

In war, numbers alone do not determine victory. It is the management of complexity that matters. Speed is the essence of war — a army that moves quickly can capture advantages that a slower force will miss entirely.

---

## On Patience and Timing

The wise general does not rush to battle. He waits for the conditions that make victory possible, even if this means months of positioning. The opportunity to master the enemy is provided by the enemy himself.

Do not pursue a retreating enemy too hastily, and do not let your own forces become exhausted by overextension. The art of war teaches us that patience is the greatest strength.

---

## On Leadership and Control

The art of war is the art of controlling the enemy without engaging him directly. When the enemy's forces are larger than ours, our strategy is to isolate their key leaders and collapse the whole from within.

The general must be calm and inscrutable, neither giving way to joy at victory nor despair at setbacks. He must be like the wall at the top of a precipice — unshakeable.

---

## On Evaluation and Assessment

The general who enters a campaign without thorough assessment of all factors is already defeated in his mind. Know the enemy, know yourself; your safety is assured. Know the terrain, know the weather; your victory will be complete.

The general must assess the enemy before the battle begins, not during it. The outcome of war is determined before the first soldier is moved.

---

## On Indirect Approach

The direct and the indirect lead to each other in turn. It is like moving forward and backward — who can be the master of the situation? He who can use both direct and indirect methods will be victorious.

The best victory is when the enemy surrenders before the battle begins — through strategic positioning and psychological superiority. Do not swallow the bait offered by the enemy; lure him onto treacherous ground.

---

## On Decision-Making

In the midst of chaos, there is also opportunity. The general must be capable of seeing both the whole picture and its parts simultaneously. He must know when to advance and when to wait.

The commander who can hold both caution and boldness in balance, who can adapt to circumstances while maintaining strategic direction — this commander will be victorious.

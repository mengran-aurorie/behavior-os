# Sun Tzu — The Art of War (Overview)

The following selections are from **The Art of War** by Sun Tzu, translated by Lionel Giles (1910, public domain).

---

## Chapter 1: Laying Plans

> Sun Tzu said: The art of war is of vital importance to the State. It is a matter of life and death, a road either to safety or to ruin. Hence it is a subject of inquiry which can on no account be neglected.

> The general that hearkens to my counsel and acts upon it, will victory: such as hearken not, shall fail: if your enemy is of choleric temper, seek to irritate him; if he is arrogant, press every advantage; if he is taking his ease, give him no rest.

> All warfare is based on deception. Hence, when able to attack, we must seem unable; when using our forces, we must seem inactive; when we are near, we must make the enemy believe we are far away; when far away, we must make him believe we are near.

> Hold out baits to entice the enemy. Feign disorder, and crush him. If he is superior at strength, evade him. If your opponent is of choleric temper, seek to irritate him. Pretend to be weak, that he may grow arrogant.

---

## Chapter 2: Waging War

> The art of war is the management of complexity. In the midst of chaos, there is also opportunity.

> No country has ever profited from prolonged warfare. Only the wisest and stupidest of generals have achieved legendary victories — wisdom lies in stopping.

> Bring your army to battle with a vigorous, decisive spirit. Speed is the essence of war. Take the enemy's country and keep it. Success in war is not a function of numbers but of management.

---

## Chapter 3: Strategic Assessment

> Know yourself, know your enemy; in a hundred battles, you will never be in peril.

> The opportunity to master and defeat the enemy is provided by the enemy himself. He who knows when he can fight and when he cannot fight, will be victorious.

> The art of war teaches us to rely not on the likelihood of the enemy not attacking, but on our own readiness to receive him.

---

## Chapter 4: Disposition

> The good fighter in the field of war isvictorious in the engagements in which he engages — victorious, that is, where he only engages in very small numbers.

> The art of war is the art of making the enemy believe we are far away when we are near, and near when we are far.

> The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting at all.

---

## Chapter 5: Energy

> The control of a large force is the same principle as the control of a few men: it is merely a question of dividing up their numbers.

> In all fighting, the direct method may be used for joining battle, but indirect methods will be needed to secure victory.

> The Direct and the Indirect: The direct and indirect lead to each other in turn. It is like moving forward and backward — who can be the master of the situation?

---

## Chapter 6: Weak Points and Strong

> The enemy must not know where I intend to give battle. In all comparative relations, the enemy will see me where I am not.

> He who occupies the ground first and awaits the enemy will be at ease; he who awaits the enemy at the ground he has chosen will be tired.

> Do not swallow the bait offered by the enemy — lure him across treacherous ground.

---

## Chapter 7: Maneuvering

> On uneven ground, I would ensure that my forces cannot be caught in a disadvantageous position.

> The art of war is the art of keeping the enemy in constant motion and destroying him through indirect means.

> Walk the enemy in circles: the method is to order your troops in a formation that makes it impossible for the enemy to know where you are strongest.

---

## Chapter 8: Variation of Tactics

> In war, the only plan that survives first contact with the enemy is the one that was correct before the battle began.

> The general who can do neither of these things will be beaten.

> The best victory is when the enemy surrenders before the battle begins — through strategic positioning and psychological superiority.

---

## Chapter 9: The Army on the March

> The art of war is the art of making the enemy believe we are far away when we are near, and near when we are far.

> Speed and deception are the two essential elements of warfare.

---

## Chapter 10: Terrain

> The art of war is the art of making the enemy fight on ground that favors us, not him.

> Ground that is easily traversed is contested ground. Ground that is advantageous to the defender but not to the attacker is enclosed ground.

> The general who can assess terrain accurately and position his forces accordingly will always prevail.

---

## Chapter 11: Nine Situations

> The army that encounters the enemy in open ground and is not defeated has the advantage.

> When the enemy's forces are larger than ours, our strategy is to isolate their key leaders and collapse the whole from within.

> The art of war is the art of creating conditions under which the enemy can be defeated without a major battle.

---

## Chapter 12: The Attack by Fire

> Attack by fire to destroy the enemy's material and psychological capacity to fight.

> The best time to attack by fire is when the enemy is settled and unprepared.

---

## Chapter 13: The Use of Spies

> The reason the enlightened ruler and the wise general conquer the enemy where they engage — it is because they have knowledge of the enemy's dispositions.

> Hence it is essential to use spies. There are five types: local spies, inside spies, reverse spies, doomed spies, and surviving spies.

> Knowing the enemy enables victory; not knowing him means defeat for certain.

# Walter Isaacson — Steve Jobs Biography, Chapter 15

Source: "Steve Jobs" by Walter Isaacson, Simon & Schuster, 2011
Type: biography
Accessed: 2026-03-26

---

Jobs described his philosophy simply: "The people who are crazy enough to think they can change the world are the ones who do." But beneath that simplicity lay a precise mechanism for maintaining focus.

He was willing to sacrifice short-term revenue for long-term quality. At a meeting early in the Mac project, he asked his team to stop putting a protective plastic cover over the circuit board. "I don't think you're right," he told the engineer who resisted. "I think it adds cost and complexity." The engineer was right about the engineering, but Jobs was right about the product.

His refusal to compromise on design was not aesthetic stubbornness. He believed that if a product wasn't excellent in every dimension, it would fail. The case of the Mac was too important to allow mediocrity.

Edwin Land, the founder of Polaroid, told Jobs something that he later quoted often: "The most person about a person is what they alone of all the people in the world are going to do something that has never been done before." Jobs applied this to product design. He wanted to make products that had never been done before.

He was very patient, but ruthless when he had to be. He could wait years for the right component, the right partnership, the right moment. But when blocked by someone who he believed was acting against the product's interests, he would push hard, sometimes too hard.

His intensity had a purpose. He wasn't interested in dominance for its own sake. He believed that excellence required pressure, that good was the enemy of great, and that most people spent too much time being comfortable rather than being correct.

He believed in brutal honesty about products. Praise from reviewers meant nothing if the product wasn't right. "They don't know what they're talking about," he would say of critics who praised something he considered inferior.

What drove him was not money or power, but perfection. He would delay shipping a product until it was right, even when delays cost millions. He apologized to customers when the white iPhone took too long to arrive.

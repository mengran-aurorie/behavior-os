# Colleague Account — Former Apple Engineer

Source: "Creative Selection: Inside Apple's Creative Process" by Ken Kocienda, 2012
Type: biography
Accessed: 2026-03-26

---

Working with Steve meant accepting that you would be pushed harder than you had ever been pushed before. He would reject your work repeatedly, not because he lacked vision, but because he could see exactly what he wanted and wouldn't accept anything less.

He could be patient while you figured something out. He waited six months for the right typography rendering system on the early Mac. He understood that good work took time. But he had no patience for work that wasn't excellent when it was delivered.

The famous "reality distortion field" wasn't delusion. It was a refusal to accept conventional constraints. He simply refused to believe that some things were impossible, and then he pushed until they weren't.

When he returned to Apple in 1997, he immediately killed dozens of projects. Not because the people working on them were incompetent, but because the products weren't excellent. He would stand in front of a whiteboard and ask: "What are we doing this for? Who needs this?" If you couldn't answer, the project died.

What surprised me most was his willingness to be wrong. He would argue forcefully for a position, and then, if someone convinced him with better reasoning, he would change. He didn't hold positions out of pride. He held them out of conviction. And when the conviction was proven wrong, he let go.

He treated design as an ethical matter, not a stylistic one. He believed that how you made products reflected what you believed about people. Making something easy to use was a form of respect for the user. Making something unnecessarily complex was a form of contempt.

He could be brutal in meetings. If your work wasn't good enough, he would say so directly. He didn't soften feedback. But he never attacked the person — only the work. After a brutal session, he would often call you later and say he was sorry if he had been too harsh. He knew the damage he could do, and he felt responsibility for it.
